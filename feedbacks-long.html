freedom of speech is essential in a democracy because it ensures that the government does not become the arbiter of the acceptable viewpoint at any given time. Strossen points out how vital this principle has been in defending not just what society might now classify as ‘hate speech’ such as racism but also pro-civil-rights messages. Strossen cites the 1972 Chicago v Mosley case, in which an African-American postal employee was only allowed to protest outside a high school, with a sign that read ‘Jones High School practices black discrimination’, on the basis of the First Amendment.

2. Hate Speech laws undermine the rights of minorities.
Strossen goes on to show how hate-speech laws can often end up targeting the very minority groups such laws purport to protect. Examples include that of a prominent journalist and member of the Uzbek minority in Kyrgyzstan who was charged with ‘inciting ethnic hatred’ for reporting on conflicts between Uzbeks and the majority Kygryz.

We can see the likelihood of this happening if we look at cases like that of Maya Forstater, whose lack of belief in innate gender identity and her belief that biological sex is real was pronounced a belief unworthy of respect in a civilised society.

As the ex-CEO of Index on Censorship, Jodie Ginsberg, said in 2016:
"You need look no further than the discussions within the same Human Rights Council for evidence of the way in which hate speech laws are easily manipulated to target those with opposing view rather than protect minorities. Combating hate speech requires a change in the societal attitudes that give rise to discriminatory views. Prohibiting speech is a poor, if not counterproductive, means of achieving that goal.”

I ask that you rethink this proposal and recommit to the principle of robust protection for freedom of speech.

The idea to make hate speech and hate crime “EU crimes” according to Art. 83 TFEU is problematic for several reasons. First, these concepts are notoriously difficult to define. The Commission does not suggest a definition nor would, in all likelihood, one be included in the text of Art. 83 TFEU. This is particularly problematic for hate speech since provisions that criminalise certain forms of speech by definition interfere with a fundamental right, namely freedom of expression (Art. 11 CFR). In that regard, hate speech is fundamentally different from all the EU crimes already included in Art. 83 TFEU. As the ECtHR has put it in Handyside, freedom of expression also includes the right to offend, shock and disturb. Any interference with that right must be provided for by law (Art. 52 para. 1 CFR), which entails that the legal basis must be sufficiently clear and precise. Whether a provision targeting hate speech can fulfil that is doubtful. In any case, there is a clear risk of self-censorship, i.e. that people would refrain from exercising their freedom of expression because the are unsure about the limits of “hate speech”.

While hate speech can, of course, have a cross-border dimension by virtue of being carried out online, this is by no means always the case. I am fundamentally opposed to any attempts of curbing the free speech, even for seemingly worthy reasons such as protection of marginalized groups. The devil, as always, lies in the details, namely, the definitions. Hate speech is defined as form of expression intend to vilify, humiliate, or incite hatred against certain group. That is very vague. Any negative comment can be made to fit such a definition. Especially if the group is disproportionately represented in crime statistics etc.
Generally speaking we might agree we should have right to criticize anyone, or any group. But by which criteria should we differentiate between mere criticism and hate speech? Can you define such objective criteria?
Yes, it is easy to think of some clear-cut examples of each, but there is a broad gray area, when the subsumption is difficult and necessarily subjective. There is a legal principle, nulla poena sine lege certa, no penalty without a certain law. Vaguely defined crimes are a deathblow to a freedom – for example famous Article 58 in USSR penal code. Democratic states that respect the rule of law cannot get even close to use such a vague terms in its criminal law. Yes, you will always have some small gray area, but it cannot be too broad. If we don’t know by which behaviour we will actually break the law, we have no legal certainty, which is cornerstone of the rule of law. Freedom of speech is there to protect controversial, even ugly speech. You can’t legitimately have a situation, when on one hand the law protects your controversial speech, and on the other hand you will be prosecuted for crossing the line of acceptable speech, when you don’t really know where the line is set (and the line is possibly very broad). Advice “if you’re not sure, then don’t say it” is not acceptable to me, if the words “freedom of speech” are to have any meaning at all.
In my mind, the term “hate speech” is somewhat comparable to “non-constructive criticism”. Can we deny that some empty, futile complaining that deserves to be called “non-constructive criticism”, can and does exist? Of course not. But the term is so vague, that almost any form of undesirable criticism can be cast aside as “non-constructive”. The danger of rejecting necessary, healthy criticism on those ground is so high, that banning “non-constructive” criticism should not be allowed.
To give the state broad opportunity to prosecute “hate speech” defined as such is to make sure the power will be abused eventually. That which can be easily abused, will be abused, that is ever valid political axiom. It can be easily used to stifle public debate about certain minorities. To ordinary person, even a mere prosecution is a punishment in itself, even if it results in exoneration. It is a public warning to everyone, who is thinking about engaging in controversial debates like that: “Do you want to have such problems? No? So shut up.” The state shouldn’t have such a power to stifle public debate like that.
My question to all of you who are prone to support this initiative: Can you honestly define hate speech in such terms as to avoid all the problems I have described? And don’t tell me it is for the lawyers. Most people are not lawyers. (I am a lawyer, and I still don’t really know how to define hate speech in such a way that clearly differentiates it from “normal” criticism.) We have a principle "ignorance of the law excuses not”. At least when it comes to criminal law, it should be strongly adhered to. The main purpose for this principle is to avoid absurd defenses like “but Your Honor, I didn’t know it is illegal to rape and murder.” Knowing the law must mean knowing what I am and am not allowed to do. Ordinary people shouldn’t need to have a lawyer to know what is and is not a crime. And when it comes to the hate speech laws, that is exactly what they need. Without a lawyer, and possible even with him, they cannot be sure, what they are and are not allowed to say. That is not acceptable.

The European Roma Grassroot Organisation Network (ERGO Network) welcomes the initiative of the European Commission to include hate speech and hate crime on the grounds of sex, sexual orientation, age and disability in the list of EU crimes in Article 83(1) TFEU.
Despite lack of systematic monitoring and collection of data on hate speech and hate crime on the grounds of sex, sexual orientation, age and disability, existing reports, suggest that LGBTI people and persons with disabilities suffer disproportionately from hate crime, hate speech and violence.
However, currently, the EU law does not require its Member States to recognise sexual orientation, gender identity and/or gender expression as a bias motivating factor in criminal law. The EU only recognises racism and xenophobia in its 2008 Framework decision on combating racism and xenophobia by means of criminal law. However, under EU law, age, disability, gender, gender identity, sexual orientation and social or economic status are protected characteristics. Article 21 of the Charter of Fundamental Rights of the European Union prohibits discrimination based on an open-ended list of grounds, including, disability, sexual orientation and social origin. In addition, the EU’s Directive on the rights of victims recognises that the nature of bias crimes and the victim’s personal characteristics, including sexual orientation, gender identity and gender expression, must be considered together with the type or nature and the circumstances of the crime to determine whether it is a hate crime, a bias crime or a crime committed with a discriminatory motive.
Due to these legislative gaps, an attack against a transexual woman who is targeted with homophobic slurs and subsequently beaten up on the street will be recorded, investigated, and prosecuted as a common crime. A similar attack suffered by a black woman and associated with racist slurs can be recorded, investigated and prosecuted as a hate crime.
The adoption of EU laws addressing hate crimes motivated on these and other grounds would bridge the protection gap at the national level, by obliging all Member States to investigate and prosecute incitement to violence or hatred on grounds such disability, gender identity or sexual orientation as criminal offences. It should also further enable law enforcement and judicial authorities to also record, investigate and prosecute hate speech and hate crime from multiple or intersecting grounds, especially in the case of Roma and LGBTI persons, who are routinely subject to hate speech and hate crimes. Existing data shows that hate crime laws and practices view and address protected categories as single and separated entities. For examples, police officers tend to choose only category of hate speech or hate crime, often based on their lack understanding of how to investigate the intersectionality of hate crimes or using the victim’s perception of the crime or their own perception of the victim’s (affected) identity- especially if the victim is a trans woman.
ERGO Network calls on the EU to extend the list of EU crimes to cover hate speech and hate crime to cover the grounds of disability, age, gender and sexual orientation and involve LGBTI persons, elderly and persons with disability in the design, development, implementation and monitoring of initiatives related to combatting hate speech and hate crime as well as improve ways, tools and mechanisms for monitoring, reporting and collecting data disaggregated by bias motivation, included by sex, sexual orientation, age and disability. Further guidelines should be developed for Member States on how to employ an intersectional approach in legislation and state responses to hate speech and hate crime.

The NCTN meets the criteria of both a civic organisation and, more relevantly, an expert network with extensive knowledge of certain areas of hate crime and radicalisation.

Of particular importance is the cross-discipline operational expertise of the NCTN in collaboration with the IACSP and other relevant strategic partners. The ability to extend research beyond the limits of a purely academic scope offers a distinct competitive edge for this call.

2) assessing the incidence, prevalence as well as potential for domestic terrorism in the sphere of hate crime originating in left-wing radicalism;

3) assessing the impact on fragmentation and polarisation of society caused by the proposed measures; and
